The Physics of Acceleration

Constant Acceleration

When an object experiences constant acceleration, its velocity increases linearly with time. This is described by the equation:

v(t) = a · t

where a is acceleration, t is time, and v(t) is velocity at time t.

Velocity-Time Graph

The velocity-time graph for constant acceleration is a straight line with slope equal to acceleration. The area under this curve represents the distance traveled.

Area = ½ · base · height = ½ · t · (a·t) = ½ · a · t²

Distance Traveled

The distance traveled by an object under constant acceleration starting from rest is given by:

y(t) = ½ · a · t²

This is exactly the area of the triangle under the velocity-time curve.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

Newton's fundamental theorem connects the concept of derivatives (rates of change) with integrals (accumulated areas):

∫ v(t) dt = y(t)

This means that the integral (area under the curve) of the velocity function gives us the distance function. Conversely, the derivative of the distance function gives the velocity function.

Historical Insight

Scholars in the Middle Ages like William Heytesbury and Nicole Oresme understood these relationships centuries before calculus was formalized. Galileo later confirmed these principles experimentally with falling objects.
